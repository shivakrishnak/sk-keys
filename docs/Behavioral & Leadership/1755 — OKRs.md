# 1755 — OKRs

⚡ TL;DR — OKRs (Objectives and Key Results) are a goal-setting framework that pairs an inspiring qualitative Objective ("what we want to achieve") with 3–5 measurable Key Results ("how we know we've achieved it") — creating aligned, ambitious goals across teams and organisations while distinguishing between output metrics (what we built) and outcome metrics (what changed for users or the business).

---

### 🔥 The Problem This Solves

**WORLD WITHOUT IT:**
Teams work hard on many things and cannot articulate what they are trying to achieve or how they would know they'd succeeded. Engineering builds features; product ships releases; the company grows (or doesn't) — and nobody is certain which work caused which outcomes. Teams optimise for their own metrics (velocity, features shipped, uptime) that may not connect to business outcomes (revenue, user retention, conversion). Quarterly planning produces project lists rather than outcome commitments. At quarter end: "we shipped everything planned" but the needle hasn't moved on the metric that matters.

**THE BREAKING POINT:**
Without a shared framework for goal-setting, every team defines success differently. The absence of shared outcome metrics means cross-team work cannot be prioritised by impact — everything is equally important because everything is measured differently. OKRs solve the coordination problem: shared Objectives create alignment; measurable Key Results create accountability.

**THE INVENTION MOMENT:**
Andy Grove invented MBOs (Management by Objectives) at Intel in the 1970s. John Doerr adapted and popularised OKRs at Google (1999 — Google's first year), describing them in "Measure What Matters" (2018). Google credits OKRs as a significant factor in its growth from 40 employees to 60,000+.

---

### 📘 Textbook Definition

**Objective:** A qualitative statement of what you want to achieve. Must be: inspiring, meaningful, action-oriented, time-bound (usually quarterly or annually). NOT a metric; NOT a task. Example: "Make our checkout experience the fastest in the industry."

**Key Results:** 2–5 quantitative measures that define what achieving the Objective looks like. Must be: specific, measurable, time-bound, and owned. Key Results can be outcomes (metrics that change) or milestones (artefacts that are delivered). Example: "Reduce P95 checkout time from 4s to <1s by March 31."

**OKR properties:**

- OKRs are not performance reviews — 70% attainment of an OKR is considered healthy (indicates appropriate ambition)
- OKRs cascade: company OKRs → team OKRs → individual OKRs (each level supports the level above)
- OKRs should be transparent and public within the organisation
- OKRs should be set collaboratively, not top-down mandated
- A team should have 3–5 Objectives with 3–5 Key Results each — fewer is better

**Committed vs Aspirational OKRs:**

- **Committed OKRs:** must be achieved; failure is not acceptable (e.g., regulatory compliance, reliability SLAs)
- **Aspirational (moonshot) OKRs:** ambitious; 70% achievement is success; 100% may indicate insufficient ambition

### ⏱️ Understand It in 30 Seconds

**One line:**
OKRs define where you're going (Objective) and how you'll know you've arrived (Key Results) — replacing activity metrics ("we shipped N features") with outcome metrics ("checkout abandonment dropped by 20%").

**One analogy:**

> OKRs are like GPS navigation with arrival confirmation. The Objective is the destination: "Get to San Francisco." The Key Results are the confirmation criteria: "Arrive before 3pm; parking in the garage on Mission St; fuel gauge above quarter." Without Key Results, you might claim you "reached San Francisco" when you're in a suburb 30 miles away. Without an Objective, the team optimises for individual metrics that don't necessarily lead anywhere useful: "I drove very fast" (velocity), "I stayed on highways" (process compliance), "I stopped for no traffic lights" (efficiency) — but nobody checked if the destination was reached. OKRs ensure both direction (Objective) and arrival confirmation (Key Results) are defined upfront.

**One insight:**
The most common OKR mistake is writing Key Results as tasks ("ship the checkout redesign") rather than outcomes ("reduce checkout abandonment by 20%"). Task-based Key Results measure what was done, not what changed. If you ship the redesign and abandonment doesn't drop, the OKR is failed — the key result is the outcome, not the delivery.

### 🔩 First Principles Explanation

**OBJECTIVE QUALITY CHECKLIST:**

```
GOOD OBJECTIVES:
  ✓ "Make our API the fastest in the competitive set"
  ✓ "Eliminate toil from the on-call rotation"
  ✓ "Enable enterprise customers to self-serve onboarding"

BAD OBJECTIVES:
  ✗ "Improve performance" — vague; not inspiring
  ✗ "Complete the auth migration" — that's a task
  ✗ "Maintain 99.9% uptime" — that's a committed KR, not an objective
  ✗ "Increase revenue by 15%" — that's a company OKR; not a team objective
                                 (unless you're a revenue team)

TEST: Can someone who doesn't work on your team understand
      why this Objective matters? Would they be inspired by it?
      If not: it's not a good Objective.
```

**KEY RESULT QUALITY:**

```
GOOD KEY RESULTS (outcomes):
  ✓ "P95 API latency < 500ms by March 31" (measurable outcome)
  ✓ "Enterprise customer onboarding time ≤ 1 day (from 2 weeks)"
  ✓ "On-call tickets/week drops from 47 to < 10"

BAD KEY RESULTS (outputs/tasks):
  ✗ "Ship the performance improvement PR" — task
  ✗ "Complete the onboarding redesign" — output
  ✗ "Implement runbook for 20 on-call scenarios" — activity

DISTINCTION:
  Output: "We shipped feature X"
  Outcome: "Feature X caused metric Y to improve by Z%"

  OKRs measure outcomes, not outputs.
  You can complete every output and fail every OKR.
```

**OKR CASCADE:**

```
Company OKR:
  O: Become the go-to platform for enterprise data workflows
  KR1: Enterprise ARR grows from $2M to $5M by Q4
  KR2: Customer NPS > 60 by Q4
  KR3: 90% of enterprise customers using data pipeline product

  ↓ Engineering Team OKR (supports Company KR3):
  O: Make data pipeline reliable and fast enough for
     enterprise production use
  KR1: Pipeline P99 latency < 2s (from 8s)
  KR2: Pipeline availability 99.9% (from 97.2%)
  KR3: Onboarding time for new pipeline < 30 min (from 4 days)

  ↓ Platform Pod OKR (supports Team KR1 and KR2):
  O: Eliminate the performance bottlenecks blocking enterprise adoption
  KR1: Identify and fix top 5 p99 latency causes by week 4
  KR2: Deploy autoscaling policy by week 6
  KR3: Replay throughput > 100k events/sec (from 8k)
```

### 🧪 Thought Experiment

**SETUP:**
Two teams. Both receive the same company mandate: "Improve developer experience."

**Team A (task-based):**
KR1: "Ship improved documentation for 10 APIs"
KR2: "Redesign the developer portal UI"
KR3: "Hold 3 developer workshops"

At quarter end: all 3 KRs complete. Developer experience unchanged — developer NPS is still 23 (poor). The team declares success because they completed the tasks.

**Team B (outcome-based OKRs):**
O: "Make the developer experience so good that developers recommend our platform to colleagues"
KR1: "Developer NPS improves from 23 to 50 by end of quarter"
KR2: "Time-to-first-API-call drops from 4 hours to 15 minutes"
KR3: "% of developers who return after first use: 42% → 70%"

At quarter end: KR1 improved to 41 (short of 50); KR2 dropped to 35 min (short of 15); KR3 reached 61%. Team achieved 70–80% of their OKRs. This is healthy ambition.

**The insight:** Team A shipped things and changed nothing. Team B moved the metrics toward a real outcome and learned which interventions worked. The OKR framework directed Team B's effort toward outcomes, not activities — and the 70% attainment gave the team useful data for the next quarter's planning.

### 🧠 Mental Model / Analogy

> OKRs separate the map from the route. The Objective is the destination on the map (the outcome you want). The Key Results are the GPS turn-by-turn coordinates that confirm you're heading in the right direction (intermediate measurable outcomes). The specific roads you take to get there (the features you build, the projects you run) are not the OKR — they are the means. A team that reports "we drove 500 miles" (we shipped 12 features) without checking whether those miles moved toward the destination (did the metric move?) is measuring roads, not destinations. OKRs force the team to check: are we getting closer to where we're trying to go?

### 📶 Gradual Depth — Four Levels

**Level 1 — What it is (anyone can understand):**
OKRs are a way for teams to set goals. The Objective is what you want to achieve (something ambitious and inspiring). The Key Results are how you'll know you got there (specific, measurable numbers that change). The key point: Key Results measure outcomes (what changed for users or the business) not outputs (what was built or shipped).

**Level 2 — How to use it (engineer):**
When contributing to OKR planning: push back on task-based Key Results. "Ship the dashboard redesign by March 31" is not a Key Result — it's a delivery milestone. "Dashboard user retention improves from 23% to 45%" is a Key Result. Ask: "If we deliver everything we planned but the metric doesn't move, have we succeeded?" If the answer is "yes," the Key Results are outputs. Rewrite them as outcomes.

**Level 3 — How it works (tech lead):**
At quarter planning: start with the company or product OKRs. For each company KR: "Which team's work moves this?" Derive your team OKRs from the company OKRs your team is best positioned to affect. Write 3–5 Objectives with 3–5 Key Results each. At weekly check-ins (usually "confidence score" 0–10): assess each KR's current trajectory. If a KR is off-track: what is blocking it? Adjust work to address the blocker. OKRs are not set-and-forget; they are weekly management instruments.

**Level 4 — Why it was designed this way (principal/staff):**
OKRs solve an information problem in large organisations: how do teams align their work to company strategy without constant top-down direction? The cascade mechanism is the answer — each level's OKRs derive from the level above, creating alignment through shared goal-setting rather than through command. The outcome (not output) requirement is the key innovation: it forces teams to measure impact, not activity, which prevents the activity trap (teams that are very busy doing things that don't matter). At the staff level, the key skill is writing Key Results that are genuinely measurable, genuinely causal (moving this metric actually means we achieved the Objective), and calibrated for ambition (70% achievement means the goal was appropriately ambitious — not too safe, not unreachable).

### ⚙️ How It Works (Mechanism)

```
OKR QUARTERLY CYCLE:

QUARTER START (week 1–2):
  Set OKRs collaboratively
  Cascade from company → team → pod
  Publish transparently across org
    ↓
WEEKLY:
  Check in: rate each KR confidence (0–10)
  Identify at-risk KRs; adjust work accordingly
    ↓
MID-QUARTER (week 6–7):
  Formal mid-quarter review
  Drop/modify OKRs that are clearly unreachable or no longer relevant
  Double down on high-progress KRs
    ↓
QUARTER END (week 12–13):
  Score each KR: 0.0–1.0
  0.7–0.8 = healthy; 1.0 = may have been too easy;
  < 0.4 = missed; root cause analysis needed
    ↓
RETROSPECT:
  Which OKRs drove the most valuable work?
  Which didn't matter at quarter end?
  What would we do differently?
    ↓
NEXT QUARTER: set new OKRs (informed by retrospect)
```

### 💻 Code Example

**OKR tracker:**

```python
from dataclasses import dataclass, field

@dataclass
class KeyResult:
    description: str
    target: float
    current: float = 0.0
    unit: str = ""

    @property
    def progress(self) -> float:
        if self.target == 0:
            return 0.0
        return min(round(self.current / self.target, 2), 1.0)

    @property
    def confidence(self) -> str:
        p = self.progress
        if p >= 0.7:
            return "On track 🟢"
        elif p >= 0.4:
            return "At risk 🟡"
        return "Off track 🔴"

    def status_line(self) -> str:
        return (
            f"  {self.description}: "
            f"{self.current}{self.unit} / {self.target}{self.unit} "
            f"({self.progress:.0%}) — {self.confidence}"
        )

@dataclass
class Objective:
    title: str
    key_results: list[KeyResult] = field(default_factory=list)

    @property
    def overall_progress(self) -> float:
        if not self.key_results:
            return 0.0
        return round(
            sum(kr.progress for kr in self.key_results)
            / len(self.key_results), 2
        )

    def print_status(self) -> None:
        print(f"\nO: {self.title}")
        print(f"   Overall: {self.overall_progress:.0%}")
        for kr in self.key_results:
            print(kr.status_line())

Objective(
    title="Make checkout the fastest in the industry",
    key_results=[
        KeyResult("P95 checkout latency", target=1.0,
                  current=1.8, unit="s"),
        KeyResult("Checkout abandonment rate", target=15.0,
                  current=22.0, unit="%"),  # lower is better — invert logic as needed
        KeyResult("Mobile checkout completion", target=80.0,
                  current=71.0, unit="%"),
    ]
).print_status()
```

### ⚠️ Common Misconceptions

| Misconception                      | Reality                                                                                                                                                                                                 |
| ---------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "100% OKR attainment = great team" | 100% consistently may mean goals were too easy. Healthy OKR attainment is 70–80%. If you always score 100%, raise the ambition.                                                                         |
| "OKRs are performance reviews"     | OKRs should be decoupled from performance reviews. If OKR scores affect compensation, teams set safe OKRs they'll definitely achieve, destroying the purpose.                                           |
| "OKRs are top-down mandated"       | Effective OKRs are negotiated: company proposes direction; teams propose specific OKRs within that direction; leadership reviews and aligns. Top-down mandated OKRs produce compliance, not commitment. |
| "Every task should map to an OKR"  | OKRs cover the top 20% of highest-impact work. Not every maintenance task, bug fix, or operational task maps to an OKR.                                                                                 |
| "OKRs replace the roadmap"         | OKRs define what outcomes to achieve. The roadmap defines which initiatives are planned to achieve them. They are complementary, not substitutes.                                                       |

### 🚨 Failure Modes & Diagnosis

**"Activity OKRs" — Key Results as Task Lists**

**Symptom:** Team OKRs for the quarter: KR1 "Ship API v2," KR2 "Complete security audit," KR3 "Migrate to Kubernetes." End of quarter: all three KRs scored 1.0. Team declares a successful quarter. Business outcome: user retention unchanged; developer NPS unchanged; zero evidence that any OKR moved a business or user metric.

**Root Cause:** Key Results were written as deliverables, not outcomes. The team measured activity (what was built) not impact (what changed). This is "activity OKRs" — technically an OKR in format but lacking the core purpose of OKRs.

**Fix:**

```
OKR WRITING WORKSHOP — rewrite activity KRs as outcome KRs:

Activity KR: "Ship API v2"
  Ask: "Why are we shipping API v2? What user/business
        outcome does it enable?"
  Answer: "Developer adoption of our API has stalled because v1
           has breaking changes and poor documentation."
  Outcome KR: "Developer API adoption: active integrations
               grow from 120 to 300 by Q3"

Activity KR: "Complete security audit"
  Ask: "What changes if the audit is complete?"
  Answer: "Enterprise customers can't sign contracts until
           SOC 2 is achieved."
  Outcome KR: "SOC 2 Type 2 certification achieved by Q3 end"
  (Note: this is a committed KR / milestone — sometimes
         milestones are valid KRs for regulatory outcomes)

Activity KR: "Migrate to Kubernetes"
  Ask: "Why Kubernetes? What outcome does it enable?"
  Answer: "We have 4h of ops toil weekly on manual scaling."
  Outcome KR: "Ops toil from manual scaling drops to < 30min/week"

RULE: For every KR, ask "so what?" until you hit a user
      or business outcome. That's your real KR.
```
