# 1747 — Feedback (Giving and Receiving)

⚡ TL;DR — Feedback is the practice of sharing specific, timely, evidence-based observations about behaviour and its impact — using frameworks like SBI (Situation-Behaviour-Impact) to make feedback actionable and non-personal — and receiving feedback with curiosity rather than defensiveness; it is a core leadership skill because it is the primary mechanism through which engineers, teams, and organisations learn and improve.

---

### 🔥 The Problem This Solves

**WORLD WITHOUT IT:**
An engineer regularly interrupts colleagues in design reviews. Colleagues find it frustrating but say nothing. The EM includes a vague note in the year-end review: "could work on communication style." The engineer is surprised and confused — this is the first time this has been mentioned. Meanwhile, for 11 months, every design review has been less effective than it could have been. The engineer, who is otherwise excellent, fails to get promoted because of a fixable behaviour that nobody told them about when it could still have mattered.

**THE BREAKING POINT:**
Without timely, specific feedback: (1) problems persist far longer than necessary, (2) the person with the problem is denied the opportunity to improve, (3) relationships deteriorate under the weight of unexpressed frustration, and (4) performance reviews become the wrong place for the first mention of longstanding issues. The absence of feedback is not kindness — it is a failure to provide the information someone needs to do their job and grow.

**THE INVENTION MOMENT:**
The SBI (Situation-Behaviour-Impact) model was developed by the Center for Creative Leadership in the 1970s as a structured alternative to vague or personal feedback. Kim Scott's "Radical Candor" (2017) popularised a two-axis feedback model that distinguishes caring personally vs. challenging directly. Carol Dweck's growth mindset research (2006) established the psychological foundation: receiving feedback productively requires the belief that one can change.

---

### 📘 Textbook Definition

**SBI Model (Situation-Behaviour-Impact):**
- **Situation:** The specific context in which the behaviour occurred ("In yesterday's design review...")
- **Behaviour:** The observable action — not interpretation or motive ("You interrupted Alice twice while she was explaining the caching approach...")
- **Impact:** The consequence of that behaviour on others, the team, or the work ("...which meant the team didn't hear her full proposal, and she didn't feel comfortable raising it again after that")

**Radical Candor (Kim Scott):** Two-dimensional model:
- Axis 1: Care personally (high vs. low) — genuine investment in the person's wellbeing and growth
- Axis 2: Challenge directly (high vs. low) — willingness to say hard things clearly
- Quadrant: High care + high challenge = Radical Candor (ideal)
- Other quadrants: Ruinous Empathy (high care, low challenge — kind but unhelpful), Obnoxious Aggression (low care, high challenge — harsh), Manipulative Insincerity (low care, low challenge — passive aggressive)

**Growth Mindset (Dweck):** The belief that abilities and intelligence are developable through effort. Enables feedback to be received as information about current state, not as a verdict on fixed character. Opposite: fixed mindset — feedback is experienced as attack on identity.

### ⏱️ Understand It in 30 Seconds

**One line:**
Effective feedback is specific, timely, evidence-based, and focused on behaviour and impact — not on character — and is received with curiosity rather than defensiveness.

**One analogy:**
> Feedback is a GPS recalculation. The GPS doesn't say "you are a bad driver for taking the wrong exit." It says: "At Junction 5 you turned south instead of north; here is the revised route." It is specific (Junction 5), descriptive (you turned south), and immediately actionable (new route). It carries no judgment about character. And the driver doesn't argue with the GPS — they use the information to navigate. Effective feedback works the same way: it tells the person specifically where they diverged from the expected path and offers a corrected route. The recipient doesn't need to feel attacked; they need to use the information.

**One insight:**
Feedback is not about the past — it is about the future. The purpose of giving someone feedback about what they did yesterday is to change what they do tomorrow. When this is the framing, the conversation becomes: "Here is information that will help you be more effective going forward" — not "here is a list of your failures."

### 🔩 First Principles Explanation

**SBI IN PRACTICE:**

```
VAGUE / BAD:
  "You need to work on your communication skills."
  Problems:
  - No specific situation: when?
  - No behaviour: what did they do?
  - No impact: what was the effect?
  - Not actionable: what should they change?
  - Feels like character attack: "you are a poor communicator"

SBI / GOOD:
  Situation:  "In yesterday's sprint planning meeting..."
  Behaviour:  "...when the junior engineers raised concerns
               about the timeline, you said 'we've already
               decided this' and moved on without engaging
               with their points..."
  Impact:     "...which left their concerns unresolved on the
               record, and I noticed they didn't raise further
               concerns for the rest of the meeting."
  
  Optional extension: "What would have looked different?"
  "Is there something about the timeline concerns that felt
   already-resolved to you that I'm missing?"
  → Opens dialogue; invites their perspective
```

**GIVING FEEDBACK — CHECKLIST:**

```
□ TIMELY: within 24–48h of the event
  Not in a performance review 6 months later

□ SPECIFIC: SBI structure
  Not vague character assessments

□ PRIVATE (corrective feedback):
  Corrective feedback → 1:1, never public
  Positive feedback → can be public, if culturally appropriate

□ EVIDENCE-BASED:
  Observable behaviour, not inferred motivation
  NOT "you don't care about quality"
  YES "the code review you submitted had 12 unbounded loops
       and no tests; that created a 3-hour review for the team"

□ GROWTH-ORIENTED:
  Framed as "here is what will make you more effective"
  Not "here is everything you did wrong"
  
□ ONE AT A TIME (corrective):
  One feedback item per conversation
  A list of corrections is overwhelming; only one sticks
```

**RECEIVING FEEDBACK — FRAMEWORK:**

```
When receiving feedback:
1. LISTEN: hear the full feedback without interrupting
2. ACKNOWLEDGE: "Thank you for sharing that — I want to
   understand it better."
3. CLARIFY: "Can you give me a specific example?"
             "What would the alternative have looked like?"
4. REFLECT: privately, after the conversation:
   - Is this accurate? (even if it stings)
   - Is this useful? (even if it's imperfectly delivered)
   - What specifically should I do differently?
5. ACT: change the behaviour
6. CLOSE THE LOOP: "I've been thinking about what you said —
   I'm going to try X differently. Does that address the concern?"

WHAT NOT TO DO:
  ✗ Defend: "But the reason I did that was..."
    (delays receiving the information; closes the conversation)
  ✗ Minimise: "It wasn't a big deal."
    (signals: don't bother giving me feedback in future)
  ✗ Counter-attack: "Well you also..."
    (weaponises feedback into argument)
  ✗ Agree falsely: "You're totally right" (but change nothing)
    (the most common and most damaging response)
```

### 🧪 Thought Experiment

**SETUP:**
You are a tech lead. Your strongest engineer, Dave, consistently delivers high-quality code but has a pattern of dismissing ideas from junior engineers in code reviews with one-line responses like "this approach won't work" — no explanation, no guidance on what would work instead. Three junior engineers have mentioned to you that they dread Dave's code reviews and are starting to avoid submitting PRs.

**What you observe:** In yesterday's code review, Dave commented "this is not the right approach" on a junior engineer's PR without further context.

**The feedback conversation:**

Situation: "Dave, I want to talk about something I noticed in yesterday's code review. In Alice's PR, you wrote 'this is not the right approach' without any additional context."

Behaviour: "I've noticed this is a pattern across several of your reviews with junior engineers — short comments that flag a problem but don't explain the thinking behind it or offer a direction."

Impact: "The impact I've seen is that Alice, Bob, and Carlos have all mentioned to me that they find your reviews difficult to learn from, and I've noticed they're taking longer to submit PRs than they used to. I think they may be avoiding the review cycle."

**Then:** "I'd love to understand your perspective — is there something about the review context that makes the detailed explanation feel unnecessary?" (opens dialogue, invites their view)

**Dave's perspective (hypothetically):** "I find it inefficient to write long comments on approaches that fundamentally don't fit the architecture — I'd rather they come to me in person."

**Resolution:** "That makes sense — the in-person conversation is great. What if we closed the loop by adding to the review comment: 'This approach has an architectural mismatch — let's discuss in person before you spend more time on it'? That preserves your efficiency and makes the path clear for them."

**The outcome:** Behaviour addresses the impact, preserves the relationship, invites their perspective, and produces an agreed alternative.

### 🧠 Mental Model / Analogy

> Feedback delivery is like error handling in code. Vague feedback is like a generic exception with no message: `Exception: something went wrong`. The programmer receives no information. Specific SBI feedback is like a well-structured exception: `ValidationError: field 'email' at line 47 does not match RFC 5322 format; expected: user@domain.tld; received: user.domain.tld`. The programmer has the exact location, the specific problem, the expected behaviour, and the actual behaviour — everything needed to fix it in one review. Effective feedback is the equivalent of a well-structured error message: specific, actionable, non-judgmental, and immediately useful.

### 📶 Gradual Depth — Four Levels

**Level 1 — What it is (anyone can understand):**
Feedback is telling someone specifically what they did, what effect it had, and what you'd like to see instead — in a way that helps them improve rather than making them feel attacked. The SBI framework (Situation, Behaviour, Impact) is a simple structure that makes feedback specific and useful.

**Level 2 — How to use it (engineer):**
Use SBI to give corrective feedback: "In [situation], when you [behaviour], the impact was [impact]." Give it within 48 hours of the event — not months later. One topic per conversation. When receiving feedback: listen without defending, ask for specific examples, and take time to reflect before responding. Even badly delivered feedback often contains useful information — your job is to extract the signal from the delivery style.

**Level 3 — How it works (tech lead):**
Build feedback into cadences: the sprint retrospective, the monthly 1:1, the code review culture. Normalise it — if feedback only happens in exceptional circumstances (annual reviews, PIPs), it becomes high-stakes and anxiety-inducing. Regular, low-stakes feedback reduces the anxiety of any individual feedback event. Model receiving feedback gracefully — thank the person publicly, share what you'll do differently. This signals to your team that feedback is safe to give.

**Level 4 — Why it was designed this way (principal/staff):**
At the principal/staff level, feedback is a systems-design problem. The question is not just "am I giving good feedback" but "does this team have the information flows it needs to improve continuously?" That requires: regular feedback cadences built into team rituals (not dependent on individual initiative), psychological safety that makes feedback safe to give upward as well as downward, feedback culture that normalises both positive reinforcement and corrective feedback (teams that only get corrective feedback develop feedback aversion), and development of team members' feedback skills (most people give feedback badly because they've never been taught how). Principal engineers invest in the feedback infrastructure of the team and org, not just their own feedback conversations.

### ⚙️ How It Works (Mechanism)

```
FEEDBACK DELIVERY:

Observe specific behaviour in specific context
    ↓
Assess: is this worth a feedback conversation?
  [yes: significant impact on others, work, or growth]
    ↓
Choose moment: private; not immediately post-event
  (give both parties time to decompress if needed)
    ↓
Open with intent: "I want to share something I observed —
                   I'm sharing it because I think it's useful."
    ↓
Deliver SBI: Situation → Behaviour → Impact
    ↓
Pause: let the person respond
    ↓
Listen: hear their perspective; don't defend your position
    ↓
Explore alternatives: "What might have looked different?"
    ↓
Close: "I'm sharing this because I think you're strong and
        this is a solvable thing. Does this land well?"
    ↓
Follow up: at next 1:1 — "How did that conversation land?
            Any thoughts since?"
```

### 🔄 The Complete Picture — End-to-End Flow

```
Behaviour observed that warrants feedback
    ↓
Reflection: what specifically is the behaviour and impact?
    ↓
Choose timing and setting (private; within 48h)
    ↓
[FEEDBACK ← YOU ARE HERE]
SBI delivery: Situation → Behaviour → Impact
    ↓
Receive response; listen to their perspective
    ↓
Dialogue: agree on alternative behaviour
    ↓
Engineer reflects; (ideally) changes behaviour
    ↓
Follow up: acknowledge improvement; close the loop
    ↓
Continuous: feedback becomes part of regular cadence
```

### 💻 Code Example

**SBI feedback constructor:**
```python
from dataclasses import dataclass

@dataclass
class Feedback:
    situation: str   # When / where did this happen
    behaviour: str   # What specifically was observed (not motive)
    impact: str      # Effect on people, work, or team
    direction: str   # "corrective" | "positive"

    def render(self) -> str:
        lines = [
            f"[Situation] {self.situation}",
            f"[Behaviour] {self.behaviour}",
            f"[Impact]    {self.impact}",
        ]
        if self.direction == "corrective":
            lines.append(
                "\n[Optional] 'What might that have looked like differently?'"
            )
        return "\n".join(lines)

    def validate(self) -> list[str]:
        issues = []
        if len(self.situation.split()) < 3:
            issues.append(
                "Situation is vague — include when/where/context")
        if any(word in self.behaviour.lower() for word in
               ("always", "never", "you are", "you're a")):
            issues.append(
                "Behaviour may contain generalisation or character "
                "assessment — focus on specific observable action")
        return issues

# Example: corrective feedback
fb = Feedback(
    situation="In yesterday's sprint planning meeting",
    behaviour="when junior engineers raised concerns about the "
              "timeline, you said 'we've already decided this' "
              "and moved on without engaging",
    impact="their concerns went unresolved and they did not "
           "raise further points for the rest of the meeting",
    direction="corrective",
)
issues = fb.validate()
if issues:
    for issue in issues:
        print(f"Warning: {issue}")
else:
    print(fb.render())
```

### ⚖️ Comparison Table

| Model | Structure | Best For | Key Limitation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **SBI** | Situation-Behaviour-Impact | Specific, timely feedback | Requires observation of specific event |
| **Radical Candor** | 2×2: Care + Challenge | Framework for feedback culture and intent | Describes attitude, not delivery mechanics |
| **Start/Stop/Continue** | Three categories | Retrospective-style group feedback | Less specific than SBI; can be vague |
| **Feedforward** | Suggestions for future (not past) | High-defensiveness situations | Doesn't address past behaviour directly |
| **360 feedback** | Multi-rater; structured | Periodic development reviews | Delayed; aggregated; less actionable than real-time |

### ⚠️ Common Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality |
|---|---|
| "Positive feedback doesn't need to be specific" | Vague positive feedback ("great work!") is not useful. Specific positive feedback ("The way you structured that RFC made the trade-offs immediately clear — it reduced the review cycle by half") reinforces the exact behaviour you want repeated. |
| "Feedback sandwich (positive-corrective-positive) works" | Research shows the positive-corrective-positive structure causes recipients to focus on the positives and dismiss the corrective element. It dilutes the feedback. |
| "Deliver feedback immediately after the event" | Immediately after a heated event, neither party has decompress time. Aim for 24–48 hours: close enough to be specific; far enough to be calm. |
| "It's unkind to give corrective feedback" | It is unkind to withhold feedback that would help someone grow. Ruinous Empathy (high care, low challenge) feels kind but leaves the person without the information they need. |
| "If feedback isn't delivered perfectly, it doesn't count" | Imperfectly delivered feedback (without SBI) can still carry useful information. The recipient's job is to extract signal from imperfect delivery. |

### 🚨 Failure Modes & Diagnosis

**The False Agree — Feedback Acknowledged and Ignored**

**Symptom:** You give specific, well-structured feedback in a 1:1. The engineer says "you're right, I'll work on that." In the next sprint, the same behaviour continues. At the following 1:1 you give the same feedback. The engineer says "absolutely, I'll be more mindful." The pattern repeats for 3 months. At year-end review, the behaviour is listed as a development area.

**Root Cause:** The engineer has learned that agreeing quickly ends the discomfort of the feedback conversation. They are not processing the feedback; they are using agreement as an escape mechanism. You have not established accountability for change.

**Fix:**
```
STEP 1: CLOSE THE LOOP IN THE CONVERSATION:
  Don't just deliver feedback → accept agreement → end.
  "What will you specifically do differently next time?
   Let's be concrete."
  Get a specific commitment: "I'll ask Alice to finish
  before I respond" rather than "I'll be more mindful."

STEP 2: SCHEDULE A FOLLOW-UP:
  "Can we check in on this at the next 1:1 in two weeks?
   I'll ask how it's going."
  → Creates accountability without surveillance

STEP 3: IF PATTERN REPEATS:
  Name the pattern explicitly:
  "I've raised this three times. Each time you agreed
   and the behaviour hasn't changed. I want to understand
   what's getting in the way — is there something about
   this feedback that isn't landing? Or something making
   change difficult?"
  → Opens dialogue on the real blocker

STEP 4: ESCALATE IF NEEDED:
  Repeated feedback cycles that produce no change
  → EM conversation: "This is a pattern I'm tracking.
    Here are the specific instances and outcomes."
  → May indicate a deeper issue: skill gap, motivation
    issue, or a behaviour that requires formal process
```

**Q1.** Kim Scott's Radical Candor model says the ideal is to "care personally and challenge directly" simultaneously. In practice, many technical leaders find one of these axes easier than the other: some are high challenge + low care (blunt, effective, but relationship-damaging), others are high care + low challenge (kind, relationship-preserving, but withhold important feedback). Which failure mode is more common in engineering cultures and why? Design a concrete practice that helps a leader who is high-care/low-challenge develop the "challenge directly" dimension.

**Q2.** You observe that a senior engineer consistently receives vague, late feedback in performance reviews — everyone knows there are concerns about his work style, but nobody has given him a direct conversation. In the current cycle, the panel is considering withholding his promotion. He does not know a promotion decision is pending. Design the feedback conversation his manager should have had 6 months ago: write the full SBI for the primary concern, the expected dialogue, and the follow-up plan. Then answer: is it ethical to withhold the promotion without having had this conversation? What does this situation tell you about the feedback culture of this organisation?
